# Mission

> ## Free yourself from repetitive work.

Anyone should be able to hand off the repetitive tasks they no longer want to
do — and have them done, on their own, around the clock.

Today, automating real work is locked behind code, CLIs, and technical skill.
Only developers and big companies get to delegate to machines. Myra's bet is
that this power belongs to **everyone**: you describe what you want done, you
delegate it, and it runs without you.

*Myra* is Swedish for *ant* — one agent is a single ant, your setup is the
colony: many workers running in parallel, quietly doing the work you'd rather
not.

## How we say it

The mission is the *why* and never changes. The lines below are how it shows up
today, as a developer-first product, and will evolve as the audience widens.

| Layer | Line | Scope |
|-------|------|-------|
| **Mission** | Free yourself from repetitive work. | The why — the world we're after. Outlives the product. |
| **Positioning** | Your AI workforce, running 24/7. | The what — how Myra presents today. |
| **Tagline** | Delegate the repetitive work. · Agents that work while you sleep. | The hook. |

These nest, they don't compete: the *workforce* is **how**; freeing yourself from
repetitive work is **why**.

## How we know we're winning

The win isn't usage — it's work that stops landing on a human. The day someone
sets up an agent, walks away, and never thinks about that task again, we've done
our job. So we hold ourselves to a handful of numbers, and only these:

- **Hours of repetitive work deleted** — the headline; everything else serves it.
  Counted from agent runs fired on a schedule, each weighted by the human time it
  replaces — estimated once, when the schedule is first set up.
- **Share of those hours from people who can't code** — proof the power left the
  terminal. Split the hours above by who ran them: self-reported at setup, or
  inferred from the work itself (a code repo plus a CLI agent reads as a
  developer; everything else doesn't).
- **Tasks still running unattended weeks later** — set once, no one babysitting.
  Measured as survival: of the schedules created in a given week, how many still
  fire a clean, untouched run a month on.
- **Agents set up once and never touched again** — they worked well enough that
  they never needed a fix. Measured as agents that keep producing clean runs with
  zero edits to their prompt, preset, or schedule after the day they were created.

Every one of these should climb, quarter over quarter. The day the
non-developer line outgrows the developer line is the day Myra stops being a
tool for developers and starts being a tool for everyone.

Two fingerprints tell us the human stepped out: a run started by the clock
rather than a hand, and a setup never edited after day one. Both say the same
thing — the work left the person and didn't come back. We only ever see it for
people who choose to share it; the rest keep their machine to themselves, and
that's the deal.
